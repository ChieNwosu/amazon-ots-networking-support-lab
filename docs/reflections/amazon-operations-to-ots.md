# From Amazon Operations to OTS: Connecting Experience to Technical Growth

## Overview

This reflection documents how working in an Amazon Fulfillment Center provides foundational awareness and transferable skills relevant to the IT Support Engineer I, Ops Tech Solutions (OTS) role. The goal is not to overclaim IT experience, but to honestly articulate how operations work builds a contextual foundation for technical support.

---

## Transferable Awareness

### Uptime and Availability Mindset

Working in an FC environment builds an intuitive understanding of why uptime matters. When systems go down, packages stop moving. This awareness translates directly to OTS work, where maintaining network and device availability is a primary responsibility. The urgency of resolving technical issues in a production environment is not theoretical when you have experienced the operational impact firsthand.

### Process Discipline

Amazon operations are built on standardized processes, metrics, and accountability. Following SOPs, documenting deviations, and communicating status are daily expectations. These same disciplines apply directly to IT support: following troubleshooting procedures, documenting actions taken, and communicating resolution status to stakeholders.

### Urgency and Prioritization

FC operations run on tight timelines with clear priorities. Understanding how to triage based on impact (what is blocking the most work right now) is a skill that transfers directly to incident prioritization in IT support. Not all issues are equal; knowing which device or system failure has the highest operational impact enables better decision-making.

### Team Coordination

Operations work is inherently collaborative. Shift handoffs, cross-functional communication, and escalation to leadership are standard practices. OTS operates similarly: coordinating with network teams, communicating with operations managers about impact, and escalating to senior engineers when issues exceed current capability.

### Physical Environment Familiarity

Understanding the FC physical layout, the types of devices in use (scanners, workstations, printers, conveyor systems), and the environmental conditions (temperature, dust, physical wear) provides context that helps an OTS engineer locate devices faster and understand failure modes that are specific to warehouse environments.

---

## What Operations Experience Does NOT Provide

Being transparent about boundaries is important:

- Operations experience does not equate to hands-on IT support experience
- Familiarity with systems as a user is different from supporting them as an engineer
- Understanding that uptime matters is different from knowing how to restore it
- Process discipline supports troubleshooting methodology, but does not replace technical knowledge

This is precisely why this learning lab exists: to bridge the gap between operational awareness and technical capability through structured coursework and deliberate practice.

---

## How This Informs My Learning Approach

1. **Runbooks are practical, not academic.** Writing troubleshooting procedures feels natural because SOPs are already part of my daily work.
2. **Uptime context motivates urgency.** Studying network fundamentals is not abstract when you have seen what happens when the network goes down on a sort floor.
3. **Documentation is valued.** Clear, scannable documentation is not optional in Amazon environments. This standard carries into technical writing.
4. **Escalation awareness exists.** Knowing when to escalate and what information to provide is a practiced skill, even before the technical content is fully developed.

---

## Summary

Amazon FC operations experience provides a strong foundation of contextual awareness, process discipline, and urgency that supports growth into an OTS technical role. The technical skills (networking, Linux, security, troubleshooting) are being built through structured coursework. This repository documents that structured growth, connecting operational awareness with developing technical capability.
